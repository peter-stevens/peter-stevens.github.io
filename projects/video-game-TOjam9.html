VIDEO GAME

Save Beagle

Here's how we achieved zero-budget motion capture for our video game.

We made Save Beagle in about 40 hours. (play it here)

It was for TOjam 9, a three day game jam. I motion captured actors and turned them into lil' pixel sprites.

Our team was five people: Andrew Gardner, Andrew Stevens, Bartek Ciszkowski, Devon Gardner and myself.

It was a heroic effort of coding, music composing, game testing, dialogue writing, parallax scrolling, and more.

Thanks to my brother, Andrew, you can watch a full spread of the animations here.

The game was about a group of animal activists distracting riot police so that one of them could escape with a rescued beagle. It was based on a true story.

It was magical how things came together. First, on a whim, I went to Value Village on Friday afternoon and found the perfect bed sheets. We had our $8 green screen.

Second, we were able to set up using a corkboard at the festival space. We were on the sixth floor of George Brown College.

Next, the actors took turns running, ducking, hopping, stumbling and more. Here's my brother in mid-clothesline.

The footage was recorded with the Mac webcam and Photobooth.

The video clips were cropped in Photoshop CS6 using File> Import> Video Frames to Layers.

The frames were moved into a single spritesheet using a nifty script for Photoshop.

The background was removed by playing with the Magic Wand and Replace Colour.

Using Image> Image Resize we set things to 30 pixels high and I changed the resize setting from Bilinear to Nearest Neighbour (to preserve hard edges).

One of my favourite moments of this game jam was late Saturday night at my brother's apartment. We set up couch pillows on the ground, improvised some riot police costumes, and went back to work.
